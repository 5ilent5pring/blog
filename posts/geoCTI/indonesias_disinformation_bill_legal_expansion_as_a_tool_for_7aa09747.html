Indonesia’s Disinformation Bill: Legal Expansion as a Tool for Domestic Political Control

> DATE: 2026-04-13 | > CATEGORY: geoCTI

Confidence: high | Triage Score: 7/10 | Category: Disinformation | Key Actors: Yusril Ihza Mahendra, Prabowo Subianto, LBH Pers, KontraS, Philippines | SAT Techniques: Argument Mapping, Devil's Advocacy, Evidence Quality Assessment

The Indonesian government, led by Coordinating Minister Yusril Ihza Mahendra, is advancing a new legislative framework to regulate 'foreign propaganda' and disinformation. This initiative bypasses existing digital regulations like the Electronic Information and Transactions Law to establish a new legal basis for state intervention in the information space. The bill's true function is to provide the state with the statutory authority to criminalize domestic dissent by reclassifying it as foreign-funded interference.

Argument Mapping

  • The author argues that the proposed disinformation bill is a mechanism for expanding state power to suppress domestic criticism rather than a genuine response to external threats.
    • The author builds this thesis on the claim that the bill lacks precise definitions for 'disinformation' and 'foreign propaganda,' allowing for arbitrary enforcement. They support this by citing the absence of the bill from the 2025–2029 National Legislation Program despite existing regulatory overlaps. A secondary claim posits that the political climate is already primed for this expansion, evidenced by President Prabowo Subianto's June 2025 claim that foreign powers fund NGOs to cause discord. The argument relies on the unstated premise that the Indonesian judiciary lacks the independence to check executive overreach during the legislative process.
    • likely

Devil's Advocacy

  • The author's argument fails to account for the possibility that the bill is a reactive, rather than proactive, attempt to address documented surges in digital information warfare.
    • The author ignores potential evidence of actual, large-scale, coordinated foreign influence operations that might necessitate new legal instruments. An alternative interpretation is that the bill is a standard regulatory response to the systemic, cross-border nature of modern disinformation mentioned in the drafting process's academic paper. The analysis also lacks the perspective of Indonesian security officials who would argue that existing laws like the Press Law are insufficient for non-human, AI-driven propaganda. The weakest link is the reliance on the Philippine 'red-tagging' analogy, which assumes the Indonesian legislative trajectory will mirror a specific, decades-long historical pattern in a different sovereign context.
    • even odds

Evidence Quality Assessment

  • The article provides a high level of empirical detail regarding domestic pressure on the press, which strengthens the author's claims regarding the current political climate.
    • The author avoids generic assertions by citing specific data from the 2025 LBH Pers Annual Report, including the 96 incidents of attacks on the press and the specific acid attack on KontraS activist Andrie Yunably on March 13. While the historical analogy to the Philippines is a subjective interpretive leap, the author anchors the argument in verifiable, recent domestic trends. The argument is rigorous because it distinguishes between the legislative intent (as stated by Mahendra) and the documented physical and legal reality for journalists.
    • rigorous and well-evidenced

What to Watch For

The author's thesis will be validated if the final text of the bill includes broad, non-specific definitions for 'foreign propaganda' that overlap with domestic NGO activities. Conversely, the thesis will be invalidated if the legislative process includes significant, codified safeguards or clear evidentiary standards for proving foreign funding in disinformation cases. Monitor the 2026 Priority Legislation Program for the specific inclusion of this bill's regulatory parameters.
